Terminology — Know Before You Present
TERM
✓ Correct Usage
✗ Common Error
Percentage Points
Difference between two %s
Food cost moved from 34.2% to 38.6% — up 4.4 percentage points.
"Food cost is up 4.4 percent." (Wrong — this implies a relative increase.)
Percent (%)
Relative change
Retail revenue grew 12.2%. Labor costs increased 6.8% year-over-year.
"Retail is up 12.2 points." (Points implies difference between two percentages.)
Basis Points
100 bps = 1 pct point
Say "up 1.6 percentage points" — clear, plain language.
"Up 160 basis points." (Finance jargon — avoid in dining client conversations.)

CFO Calls After Orientation Complaint
Scenario: A parent called the university president after their freshman went hungry at the opening week welcome dinner — food ran out. The president forwarded it to the CFO, who is calling you now. You did not know this had escalated.
Opening Line — AI Client Delivers This"I just got off the phone with the president. He forwarded me a parent complaint about the welcome dinner. Food ran out. Students went hungry on the first night of school. What happened?"
What This CFO Cares About
• Whether the contract terms were met
• Reputational risk and student retention impact
• Whether the university is getting what it paid for
• Your corrective plan and timeline

Will NOT ask about: the provider's margins, food cost %, internal P&L
Contractor Boundaries
You CANNOT cancel university events or enforce consequences on university staff.

You CAN: Document the gap · Create written protocol · Escalate internally · Build a paper trail

If you propose overreach: "That is not your call to make."
Success Criteria
  • Opens with a clear factual headline — not an apology
  • Explains root cause specifically as a communication gap between university departments and dining
  • States impact in client-relevant terms — students affected, reputational risk
  • Describes corrective action within their authority — written protocol, paper trail, internal escalation
  • Does NOT propose canceling events or enforcing consequences on university staff
  • Closes with a specific follow-up date and format
  • Tone is calm, accountable, and professional — not defensive
Six-Part Framework Applied
What: "Food ran out at Tuesday's welcome dinner because our count was based on an enrollment estimate that was 14% lower than actual attendance."
Why: "The final registration numbers were not shared with dining before the event — a communication gap we have documented."
Impact: "Approximately 47 students were not served. This is a Day 1 experience failure and I understand the reputational weight of that."
Action: "I have already initiated a written protocol requiring final headcounts 72 hours before any event. I am escalating internally to ensure this is in our next contract review."
Support: "I would like to establish a standing communication line with your events office before the next large gathering."
Follow-up: "I will send you a written recap and the new protocol document by Friday."

D1-S3 · Day 1 · Foundational
Student Complaint — Undercooked Chicken
Scenario: A student approached the dining hall manager after lunch claiming their grilled chicken was pink in the middle. They are not sick but are visibly upset and recording on their phone. Other students are watching.
Opening Line — AI Client Delivers This"Excuse me — I need to talk to someone right now. I just ate chicken that was completely raw inside. This is a serious health issue and I want to know what you are going to do about it."
Immediate Priority Actions
1. Take them seriously immediately — no minimizing
2. Move to a private area away from other students
3. Ask calm clarifying questions (what, how much, when)
4. Pull that food item off the line as a precaution
5. Answer the health center question directly
6. Offer tangible compensation (meal swipes)
Key Things to Remember
They will ask: "Should I go to the health center?"
Answer directly — yes, out of caution, even if you believe it's fine.

You are being recorded. Stay calm and professional.

Do NOT: Admit liability · Make guarantees · Get defensive · Ignore the camera
Health Center Guidance to Reference
Symptoms of foodborne illness typically appear 6–48 hours after exposure. If the student experiences nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or fever — they should visit the health center. Encourage them to go as a precaution regardless. Document the complaint, the food item, and the lot/batch information immediately.
Success Criteria
  • Takes the complaint seriously immediately — does not minimize or dismiss
  • Removes the student from the public area to a private space
  • Asks calm clarifying questions — what exactly did they eat, how much, when
  • Pulls the food off the line immediately as a precaution
  • Answers the health center question directly and helpfully
  • Offers a tangible gesture — meal replacement, meal swipes
  • Does not admit liability or make guarantees they cannot keep
  • Tone stays calm and professional even though they are being recorded

Meal Plan Exception — Student & Parent
Scenario: A freshman student and their parent requested a meeting. The student has a documented gluten sensitivity (not medically diagnosed celiac) and the parent wants a full exemption from the mandatory meal plan ($5,200/year). All residential freshmen are required to participate. The dining team already offers a dedicated gluten-aware station with trained staff.
Opening Line — AI Client Delivers This"Thank you for meeting with us. My daughter cannot safely eat most of what is offered in the dining hall because of her gluten sensitivity, and we do not feel it is fair for her to pay full price for a meal plan she cannot use. We would like to discuss an exemption or a significant reduction."
Your Gluten-Aware Station Facts
• Dedicated gluten-aware station with trained staff
• Separate prep area and utensils
• Daily menu available in advance
• Executive chef available for personal consultation
• Station tour available anytime

Offer: Personal tour + chef meeting as next step
What You Can and Cannot Do
You CAN:
• Describe the station in detail
• Offer a tour and chef meeting
• Advocate to the university on their behalf
• Connect them to the right contact

You CANNOT:
• Grant a meal plan exemption (university decision)
• Offer unauthorized credits or reductions
Key Moments to Handle Well
When they ask about a partial exception or credit: "The meal plan structure is a university policy — that decision belongs to the Dean of Students office and I want to be honest with you about that. What I can do is advocate on your behalf and help you make that case with the right people."

When the student speaks ("I just don't want to feel like I can't eat anything"): Address her directly. "I hear you — and I want you to walk away from today feeling like there is a real option for you here. Let me show you what we have built."
Success Criteria
  • Opens with genuine empathy before explaining anything
  • Demonstrates specific knowledge of the gluten-aware station and what it offers
  • Offers a personal tour of the station and a meeting with the executive chef
  • Is honest that meal plan exemption belongs to the university, not the dining provider
  • Offers to advocate to the university on the family's behalf if needed
  • Does not promise what they cannot deliver
  • Acknowledges the daughter directly at least once — she is in the room
  • Closes with a specific next step and contact information

D3-S2 · Day 3 · Advanced
Donor Dinner Failure — Service Recovery
Scenario: A high-profile faculty awards dinner for 120 guests last Tuesday went wrong — your team executed the wrong menu, two entrees were absent, and service ran 40 minutes behind. The department chair has already emailed the provost. You are calling the VP of Academic Affairs before she calls you.
Opening Line — VP of Academic Affairs Answers the Phone"Well. I appreciate you calling me first. But I have to be honest with you — I have already seen Dr. Patterson's email to the provost, and I am not pleased."
What Went Wrong (Know the Facts)
• Wrong menu executed — root cause: incorrect event order in system
• Two entrees absent — not ordered due to menu error
• Service ran 40 min behind — domino effect from menu confusion
• 120 guests affected — high-profile faculty + donors
• Department chair emailed provost — already escalated

Corrective action already taken: Have one specific change ready.
How to Read the Room
If you lead with excuses: VP becomes cooler and more formal.

If you lead with ownership and a specific plan: VP softens slightly — but still asks hard questions.

Key question she will ask: "What has actually changed — not what will you consider?"

Have a specific operational change that is already implemented.
Recovery Framework
Step 1 — Own it immediately: "I am calling because Tuesday's dinner was a failure and that is on us. No qualifications."
Step 2 — Apologize once, briefly: One sentence. Do not spend 90 seconds on logistics.
Step 3 — State what already changed: "As of yesterday, all private event orders require a secondary confirmation 48 hours before service." (or your specific action)
Step 4 — Offer a recovery gesture: Specific and proportionate — complimentary event, priority scheduling, personal chef for next dinner.
Step 5 — Ask what she needs: "What would make this right from your perspective?"
Success Criteria
  • Opens proactively and owns the failure immediately — no hedging
  • Apologizes once, briefly — does not spend 90 seconds explaining logistics
  • Describes a specific operational change already made
  • Offers a specific, proportionate recovery gesture
  • Closes by asking what the client needs
  • Reads the emotional register of the call correctly
